Early Development

The history of the internet dates back to the 1960s, when the U.S. Department of Defense developed a way to connect computers to each other in order to share information. This network, called ARPANET, was the precursor to the modern internet.

Over the next few decades, the internet continued to grow and develop, with the creation of email, file transfer protocols, and other early technologies.

The Web

In 1991, British computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee developed the World Wide Web, a system of interconnected documents that could be accessed via the internet. This invention revolutionized the way people access and share information, and laid the groundwork for the modern internet as we know it today.

Web Browsers

The first web browser was called WorldWideWeb, and it was created by Tim Berners-Lee in 1990. Other early web browsers included Mosaic and Netscape Navigator. Today, the most popular web browsers are Google Chrome, Mozilla Firefox, and Microsoft Edge.

Today

The internet has come a long way since its early days. Today, it is a global network of interconnected computers, servers, and other devices that allows people to connect with each other, share information, and access a wide variety of online services and resources.

Social Media

Social media has become a major part of the internet in recent years, with platforms like Facebook, Twitter, and Instagram allowing people to connect and share content with each other. Social media has had a profound impact on the way people communicate and interact with each other online.

E-Commerce

E-commerce, or online shopping, has also become a major part of the internet. Websites like Amazon, eBay, and Etsy have revolutionized the way people buy and sell goods and services online. Today, you can buy almost anything online, from clothes and electronics to groceries and furniture.

Streaming Services

Streaming services like Netflix, Hulu, and Disney+ have also become incredibly popular in recent years, allowing people to watch movies and TV shows on demand. These services have disrupted traditional TV and movie distribution models, and have given rise to a new era of entertainment consumption.

More Fun Facts About the Internet

In addition to the major milestones we've covered, there are plenty of interesting tidbits about the internet that you might not know:

  • The first message sent over the internet was "LO" - the intention was to send "LOGIN" but the system crashed after the first two letters were sent.
  • The first website ever created was dedicated to information about the World Wide Web itself, and went live on August 6, 1991. You can still view it today!
  • The "@" symbol was originally used to signify "at the rate of" in accounting and commercial transactions, but it became popularized as a way to denote email addresses in the 1970s.
  • The concept of the "404 error" (which occurs when a webpage cannot be found) was first introduced by a group of developers at CERN in the early days of the web.
